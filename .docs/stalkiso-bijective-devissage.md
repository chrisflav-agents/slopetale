# Dévissage: BijectiveOnStalks for local isomorphisms

This document gives a Grothendieck-style dévissage for the two main results in `Proetale/Algebra/StalkIso.lean`:

1. **`RingHom.IsLocalIso.bijectiveOnStalks`**: Local isomorphisms are bijective on stalks.
2. **`RingHom.BijectiveOnStalks.bijective_of_bijective`**: A ring homomorphism bijective on stalks with bijective Spec is itself bijective.

## Step 1: Mathematical landscape

### Available tools

- **Localization yoga**: If two ring homomorphisms out of `R` both make the target an `IsLocalization` at the same submonoid, they induce a bijection (`IsLocalization.bijective`). Composition of localRingHom's factors via `Localization.localRingHom_comp`. Bijectivity of a composition `h ∘ k` with `h` bijective implies `k` bijective (`Function.Bijective.of_comp_iff'`).
- **Standard open immersions**: A local iso `f : R →+* S` means: around every prime `p` of `S`, there is some `g ∉ p` such that `R → S_g` is a standard open immersion, i.e. `S_g ≅ R_r` for some `r ∈ R`.
- **Flatness from stalks**: A ring homomorphism is flat if all stalk maps at maximal ideals are flat (`flat_of_localizations_flat`).
- **Injectivity from stalks**: A ring homomorphism is injective if all stalk maps at maximal ideals are injective and `Spec f` is surjective (`injective_of_injectiveOnStalks`).
- **Surjectivity criterion**: `f` is surjective iff for every `s ∈ S` and every maximal ideal `m` of `R`, there exists `r ∉ m` with `f(r) · s ∈ im(f)` (`surjective_of_forall_isMaximal_exists`).
- **Generalizing maps reflect specialization when injective**: If `g : X → Y` is injective and generalizing, then `g(x) ⤳ g(y)` implies `x ⤳ y` (`GeneralizingMap.specializes_of_map_specializes`).

### Natural simplifying assumptions

For `bijectiveOnStalks`: reduce to a single prime `p`, then localize to a neighborhood where the local iso becomes a standard open immersion.

For `bijective_of_bijective`: split into injectivity and surjectivity. Injectivity is a direct application of existing API. Surjectivity requires flatness (from stalk bijectivity) and a going-down argument.

## Result 1: Local isomorphisms are bijective on stalks

**Theorem.** Let `f : R →+* S` be a local isomorphism. Then for every prime `p` of `S`, the stalk map `R_{f⁻¹(p)} → S_p` is bijective.

*Proof in 3 reductions.*

**Goal**: The stalk map `R_{f⁻¹(p)} → S_p` is bijective.

**Step 1.** We may replace `S_p` by `(S_g)_{p_g}` where `g ∉ p` makes `R → S_g` a standard open immersion. *Justification*: By definition of local iso, such a `g` exists. Set `p_g = p · S_g`. Since `g ∉ p`, the submonoid generated by `g` is disjoint from `p`, so `p_g` is prime with `p_g ∩ S = p`. The canonical map `S_p → (S_g)_{p_g}` is a bijection because both are localizations of `S` at the prime complement of `p` — localizing away from `g` first does not affect the stalk at `p` since `g` is already a unit there. By `IsLocalization.bijective`.

**Step 2.** It suffices to show that `R_{f⁻¹(p)} → (S_g)_{p_g}` is bijective. *Justification*: The stalk map `R_{f⁻¹(p)} → S_p` factors as `R_{f⁻¹(p)} → S_p → (S_g)_{p_g}` by `localRingHom_comp`. The second map is bijective (Step 1). So the first map is bijective iff the composition is, by `of_comp_iff'`.

**Step 3.** The map `R_{f⁻¹(p)} → (S_g)_{p_g}` is bijective. This is trivial. *Justification*: Since `R → S_g` is a standard open immersion, `S_g ≅ R_r` for some `r`. Therefore `(S_g)_{p_g}` is a localization of `R` at `f⁻¹(p)`. Both source and target are localizations of `R` at the same prime complement, so the map is bijective by `IsLocalization.bijective`.

## Result 2: Bijective on stalks + bijective Spec implies bijective

**Theorem.** Let `f : R →+* S` be bijective on stalks with `Spec f` a bijection. Then `f` is bijective.

*Proof in 5 reductions.*

**Goal**: `f` is bijective.

**Step 1.** It suffices to show `f` is injective and surjective separately. *Justification*: Definition of bijectivity.

**Step 2 (Injectivity).** `f` is injective. This follows from existing API. *Justification*: Stalk maps are bijective, hence injective, at all primes — in particular at all maximal ideals. Since `Spec f` is bijective, it is surjective. Apply `injective_of_injectiveOnStalks`.

**Step 3 (Surjectivity setup).** It suffices to show: for every `s ∈ S` and every maximal ideal `m` of `R`, there exists `r ∉ m` with `f(r) · s ∈ im(f)`. *Justification*: This is the criterion `surjective_of_forall_isMaximal_exists`.

**Step 4 (Flatness and going-down).** We may use that `Spec f` reflects specialization. *Justification*: `f` is flat because all stalk maps are bijective, hence flat (`flat_of_localizations_flat`). A flat ring homomorphism has generalizing `Spec` (`Flat.generalizingMap_comap`). Since `Spec f` is also injective (from bijectivity), it reflects specialization: `f⁻¹(q) ≤ f⁻¹(p)` implies `q ≤ p` (`GeneralizingMap.specializes_of_map_specializes`). Contrapositively: for primes `p, q` of `S` and `c ∈ q \ p`, we have `¬(q.comap f ≤ p.comap f)`.

**Step 5 (Clearing denominators).** Fix `s ∈ S` and a maximal ideal `m` of `R`. We produce `r ∉ m` with `f(r) · s ∈ im(f)`. This is a direct calculation. *Justification*: Since `Spec f` is surjective, find a prime `q` of `S` with `q.comap f = m`. By bijectivity of the stalk map at `q`, write `s` as a fraction: there exist `r₀ ∈ R` and `b ∈ R \ m` (i.e. `b ∉ f⁻¹(q) = m`) such that `f(b) · s = f(r₀)` modulo `q`-torsion. Precisely, there exists `c ∈ q` with `c · (f(b) · s − f(r₀)) = 0`. We claim `c` becomes a unit in the localization of `S` at `T = f(R \ m)`. If not, `c` lies in some maximal ideal `I` of this localization; pulling back, `I ∩ S` is a prime with `(I ∩ S).comap f ≤ m`. But `c ∈ q ∩ (I ∩ S)` while `c ∉ p$ for any prime `p` with `p.comap f ≤ m$ other than `q` — this contradicts the going-down property from Step 4. So `c` divides some `f(r₁)$ with `r₁ ∉ m$, giving `f(r₁) · (f(b) · s − f(r₀)) = 0`, i.e. `f(r₁ · b) · s = f(r₁ · r₀)` with `r₁ · b ∉ m`.

## Comparison with the original proof

**Strategy**: The dévissage follows the same skeleton as the original proof — there is no genuinely different route available. The original proof is already structured as a sequence of reductions, and the main mathematical ideas (localize to a standard open immersion, use `IsLocalization.bijective`, split into injectivity/surjectivity, flatness gives going-down, clear denominators) are the natural ones.

**Key differences**:
- The dévissage makes the factorization argument in `bijectiveOnStalks` more transparent by foregrounding the two localization identifications (Steps 1 and 3) before invoking the factorization (Step 2). The original proof builds all three pieces and combines them at the end.
- For `bijective_of_bijective`, the dévissage separates the going-down argument (Step 4) from the denominator-clearing calculation (Step 5), making it clearer that the going-down property is used exactly once — to show a specific element is a unit.

**Key insight**: The proof of `bijectiveOnStalks` is purely localization yoga: a local iso lets you compare stalks by routing through an intermediate localization where both source and target are localizations of the same ring at the same submonoid. The proof of `bijective_of_bijective` combines three independent tools (injectivity from stalks, flatness from stalks, surjectivity criterion via going-down) that each reduce to a standard fact.

**Assessment**: The original proof is already well-structured. The dévissage is a reorganization that makes the logical dependencies more explicit, but does not represent a fundamentally simpler argument.
